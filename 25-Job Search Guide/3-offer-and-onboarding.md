# Offer and Onboarding Guide
### Step 3 — Navigating Your First Offer and Your First 90 Days
*Evaluating Offers · Negotiating · Starting Strong · Building Trust · Growing Intentionally*

---

> **This is Step 3.** Step 1 covered how to build your presence. Step 2 covered how to navigate interviews. This guide picks up where that left off — what to do when an offer arrives, how to evaluate and negotiate it confidently, and how to show up in your first 90 days in a way that builds trust and sets you up for long-term growth.

## Overview: Why This Stage Matters

Most junior developer guides end the moment you get an offer. In reality, that's where a new set of challenges begins.

Getting the offer is one milestone. What you do next determines:

- Whether you accept the right role for the right reasons
- Whether you leave money or benefits on the table
- Whether you build trust quickly or struggle to find your footing
- Whether you grow toward mid-level intentionally or drift without direction

Most early-career engineers make the same mistakes at this stage:

- They accept the first offer without evaluating it carefully
- They don't negotiate because they're afraid to lose the offer
- They try to prove themselves by working silently instead of communicating
- They have no plan for growth beyond "just do good work"

This guide gives you a clear framework for each of those challenges.

> 💡 **"Getting the offer is the beginning, not the finish line. What you do in the first 90 days shapes your reputation for years."**

## The Offer and Onboarding Lifecycle

Most first-role experiences follow a pattern like this:

```
Offer Received
   ↓
Evaluate the Offer
   ↓
Negotiate (if applicable)
   ↓
Accept and Prepare
   ↓
First Week — Listen and Learn
   ↓
First Month — Build Relationships and Contribute
   ↓
First 90 Days — Establish Trust and Deliver
   ↓
Ongoing Growth — Move Toward Mid-Level Intentionally
```

Not every situation looks exactly like this, but most first roles follow some version of this arc.

## 1️⃣ — Evaluating the Offer

### What This Is

An offer letter is a formal document from an employer outlining the terms of your employment. It typically includes your compensation, role title, start date, and sometimes benefits.

Receiving an offer can feel exciting — and it should. But before you respond, take time to **read and understand everything in it**.

This is **not** the moment to say yes immediately.

---

### What to Look For in an Offer Letter

Most offer letters for junior software engineering roles will include:

| Component | What to Look For |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Base Salary** | Annual compensation before taxes and deductions |
| **Job Title** | Does it reflect the role you interviewed for? |
| **Start Date** | Is it realistic given your current situation? |
| **Employment Type** | Full-time, part-time, or contract? |
| **Location / Remote Policy** | In-person, hybrid, or fully remote? Is this what was discussed? |
| **Benefits** | Health, dental, vision — who pays and how much? |
| **Paid Time Off (PTO)** | How many days? Is it accrued or available immediately? |
| **Equity / Stock** | Do you receive any? If so, what type and on what schedule? |
| **Signing Bonus** | Is there one? Are there conditions attached (e.g., clawback if you leave early)? |
| **Probationary Period** | Is there a review period before you're considered fully permanent? |

> ⚠️ **Read the whole document.** Many candidates skip straight to the salary number. The full package — benefits, time off, flexibility, growth opportunities — often matters more than the base salary alone.

---

### How to Compare Two Offers

If you have multiple offers, don't just compare salaries. Look at the full picture:

| Factor | Why It Matters |
|--------|---------------|
| **Total compensation** | Salary + bonus + equity + benefits combined |
| **Learning opportunity** | Will you grow faster at one company than the other? |
| **Team and manager** | Did the people you met seem like people you'd learn from? |
| **Tech stack** | Will the work build skills that make you more employable? |
| **Company stability** | Is the company well-funded or profitable? |
| **Culture and values** | Does how they operate align with how you want to work? |
| **Career path** | Is there a clear path to promotion or growth? |

For early-career engineers, **learning opportunity often matters more than salary** — a role that pays $10k less but puts you on a great team with strong mentorship can be worth far more over a two-year horizon.

---

### It's Okay to Ask for Time

You do not need to respond to an offer the moment it arrives. It is completely professional to ask for time to review it.

A simple response:

> *"Thank you so much — I'm really excited about this opportunity. Could I have until [specific date, typically 3–5 business days] to review the details carefully?"*

Most companies expect this. Anyone who pressures you to decide within hours is showing you something important about how they operate.

### How to Prepare

- Read the entire offer letter before responding — not just the salary line
- Write down every question you have and get answers before deciding
- Research salary ranges for the role, level, and location using sites like Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, or LinkedIn Salary
- List out your non-negotiables versus nice-to-haves before you start any negotiation conversation
- Ask for time to review if you need it — 3 to 5 business days is standard and professional

## 2️⃣ — Negotiating Your Offer

### What This Is

Negotiation is the process of having a conversation with an employer about the terms of your offer before you formally accept. It is **normal, expected, and professional**.

Most hiring managers expect candidates to negotiate. Not negotiating often means leaving money or benefits on the table.

> 💡 **"Negotiating does not put your offer at risk. Companies almost never rescind an offer because a candidate asked a reasonable question."**

---

### What Junior Developers Can Realistically Negotiate

As an early-career engineer, you may have less leverage than a senior candidate — but you still have room to negotiate.

| What You Can Negotiate | Notes |
|------------------------|-------|
| **Base salary** | Even a 5–10% increase is worth asking for |
| **Start date** | If you need more time, just ask |
| **Signing bonus** | Often easier to get than a salary increase |
| **Remote / hybrid flexibility** | If it wasn't clearly specified |
| **PTO days** | Sometimes flexible, especially if the base offer is low |
| **Professional development budget** | Courses, conferences, books |
| **Equipment** | Laptop, monitor, home office stipend |
| **Review timeline** | Ask when your first performance review will be |

What is usually **not** negotiable for junior roles:
- Equity vesting schedules at established companies
- Benefits structures (these are typically company-wide)
- Job title at most companies

---

### How to Negotiate — Step by Step

**Step 1: Know your number before the conversation**

Research market rates for the role, level, and city. Know what you're asking for and why before you pick up the phone.

**Step 2: Express enthusiasm first**

Never lead with "I want more money." Lead with genuine excitement about the role.

**Step 3: Make a specific ask**

Vague asks get vague responses. Name a specific number or request.

**Step 4: Give a brief reason**

You don't owe a long explanation, but a single sentence of context helps.

**Step 5: Stop talking**

After you make your ask, stay quiet. Let them respond. Silence feels awkward but it works in your favor.

---

### Example Negotiation Script

> *"I'm really excited about this role and the team — this is exactly the kind of opportunity I've been looking for. Based on my research into the market rate for this role in [city], I was hoping we could discuss bringing the base salary up to [specific number]. Is that something there's flexibility on?"*

That's it. No lengthy justification. No apology. No ultimatum.

---

### If They Say No

A "no" to your negotiation is not a rejection of you. It is a business decision. You can respond with:

> *"I understand — I appreciate you looking into it. I'm still very excited about the role. Could we revisit compensation at my first performance review?"*

This keeps the door open and shows maturity.

### How to Prepare

- Research salary ranges before the conversation using Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, or the Bureau of Labor Statistics
- Write out your ask word-for-word and practice saying it out loud — it will feel less awkward when you do it for real
- Decide your minimum acceptable number before you negotiate so you know whether to accept or walk away
- Prepare one or two fallback requests in case salary isn't flexible — signing bonus, extra PTO, or a review date
- Remember: the worst realistic outcome is they say no and the offer stays the same

## 3️⃣ — Preparing to Start

### What This Is

The window between accepting an offer and your first day is often overlooked. How you spend it can meaningfully affect how confident and prepared you feel walking in.

This does **not** mean grinding through tutorials or memorizing documentation. It means setting yourself up mentally, practically, and professionally.

---

### What to Do Before Your First Day

**Confirm the logistics:**
- Where do you go or log in on day one?
- Who is your point of contact?
- What equipment will you receive or need to bring?
- What time does the workday typically start?

**Do light research — not deep study:**
- Read the company's website, blog, and recent news
- Understand the product at a user level — try using it if you can
- Read any public documentation or engineering blog posts they've published
- Look up a few team members on LinkedIn so faces and names feel familiar

**Prepare practically:**
- Set up your workspace if you're working remotely
- Plan your commute or login routine so day one isn't chaotic
- Get enough sleep the week before — starting exhausted makes everything harder

**Set your mindset:**

> You were hired because they believe you can do this job. Your goal in the first week is not to prove you're brilliant — it's to show that you're **curious, communicative, and coachable**.

### How to Prepare

- Reach out to your future manager or HR contact a few days before to confirm your day-one plan and ask if there's anything you should know or read in advance
- Try the product you'll be working on as an end user — understanding what you're building matters
- Write down 5 questions you're genuinely curious about going in — teams respect engineers who ask thoughtful questions from day one
- Set up your workspace, equipment, and environment before day one so you're not troubleshooting logistics when you should be paying attention

## 4️⃣ — Your First Week: Listen and Learn

### What This Is

Your first week is an information-gathering mission. You are not expected to ship code, fix major bugs, or make process improvements. You are expected to **show up, pay attention, and start building trust**.

Most new engineers feel pressure to prove themselves immediately. That pressure usually backfires — it leads to working in isolation, avoiding questions, and making avoidable mistakes.

> 💡 **"The engineers who build trust fastest in their first week are not the ones who code the most. They're the ones who ask the best questions."**

---

### What to Focus On

#### Understand the Codebase — Don't Try to Master It

Reading someone else's code is a skill of its own. Don't expect to understand everything in the first week.

Instead:
- Ask for a walkthrough of the main areas of the codebase
- Find out how to run the project locally and get it working
- Read recent pull requests to understand how the team writes and reviews code
- Ask what the most important part of the system is to understand first

#### Understand the Team's Workflow

Every team has a process. Learn it before you try to contribute to it.

- How does work get assigned? (Jira, Linear, GitHub Issues?)
- What does a typical sprint or week look like?
- How does code review work — who reviews, how long does it take?
- Where do people ask questions — Slack, in standups, async?
- What are the unwritten norms? (Camera on in meetings? Async-first culture?)

#### Build Relationships Early

The relationships you build in your first week shape how people think about you for months.

- Introduce yourself genuinely — not with a rehearsed pitch
- Remember names and use them
- Ask people about their work and what they're focused on
- Find out who your go-to person is for different types of questions

---

### First Week Checklist

- [ ] Get your local development environment fully working
- [ ] Understand how to run the main project
- [ ] Read at least 5 recent pull requests
- [ ] Attend every meeting you're invited to and take notes
- [ ] Introduce yourself to every teammate you'll work with regularly
- [ ] Find out how work is assigned and tracked
- [ ] Ask your manager: "What would a successful first month look like?"

### How to Prepare

- Write down every question that comes up during your first week — don't let questions pile up silently in your head
- At the end of each day, jot down two or three things you learned and one thing you're still unclear on — this helps you track your own progress and surfaces good questions for your manager
- Don't wait until you're completely stuck to ask for help — ask early, ask specifically, and always share what you've already tried

## 5️⃣ — Your First Month: Contribute and Connect

### What This Is

By the end of your first month, you should be making small but real contributions — shipping code, fixing bugs, or improving documentation — while continuing to build relationships and understand the product.

This is the phase where most junior engineers feel imposter syndrome most acutely. Everyone around you seems to know things you don't. That feeling is normal and almost universal. It does not mean you don't belong.

> ⚠️ **The engineers who struggle most in their first month are usually the ones who stay quiet when they're confused and work in isolation when they're stuck. Don't do this.**

---

### How to Contribute Without Overreaching

**Start with small, well-scoped tasks.**

Your first few contributions don't need to be impressive — they need to be complete, correct, and well-communicated. A small PR that's clean, tested, and explained clearly is far better than a large PR that's hard to review.

**Ask before you build.**

Before you spend days working on something, make sure you understand what "done" looks like. Ask:
- "What would a good solution to this look like?"
- "Are there parts of the codebase I should look at before starting?"
- "Is there anything I should avoid or be aware of?"

**Communicate your progress.**

Don't disappear for days and reappear with a finished feature. Update your team on where you are, what's blocking you, and what you're working on next. This is not micromanagement — it's how good teams operate.

---

### How to Read a Codebase You Didn't Write

This is one of the most underrated skills for junior engineers. Here's a practical approach:

| Step | What to Do |
|------|-----------|
| **Start at the entry point** | Find where the app starts — `index.js`, `main.py`, `App.jsx` — and read outward from there |
| **Follow the data** | Pick one user action and trace it from the UI through to the database and back |
| **Read the tests** | Tests often explain what code is supposed to do better than the code itself |
| **Read recent PRs** | Pull requests show you how the team thinks and what they care about in code review |
| **Ask for a tour** | Request a 20-minute walkthrough from a senior engineer — most are happy to give one |

---

### Building Relationships That Matter

In your first month, invest time in three types of relationships:

**Your manager** — Meet with them regularly. Share your progress honestly. Ask for feedback before you think you need it. Ask what they wish junior engineers did more of.

**Your immediate teammates** — These are the people who will review your code, answer your questions, and vouch for you in the future. Be curious about their work. Offer help when you can, even if it's just looking something up.

**Engineers on other teams** — You don't need to know everyone, but understanding how other parts of the company work makes you a better contributor to yours.

### How to Prepare

- Ask your manager at the end of week one: "What should I focus on in my first month?" — then write it down and refer back to it
- Find one recurring task or area of the codebase to own — even something small — so you build depth in at least one place
- Set up a recurring 1:1 with your manager if one isn't already scheduled — these conversations are where growth actually happens
- When you get feedback on a PR, read it carefully, ask clarifying questions if needed, and apply it — this is the fastest way to show you're coachable

## 6️⃣ — Your First 90 Days: Build Trust and Deliver

### What This Is

By the end of 90 days, you should have a clear sense of:
- How the team works and where you fit
- What you're good at and what you're still developing
- What your manager and teammates think of your work
- What you want to focus on next

This is also typically when many companies do a formal or informal check-in on new hires. How you've shown up in these 90 days shapes your reputation — and your reputation is one of the most valuable things you build in your career.

---

### What "Building Trust" Actually Looks Like

Trust at work is built through small, consistent actions over time — not one impressive moment.

| Action | Why It Builds Trust |
|--------|---------------------|
| Doing what you said you'd do | Reliability is rare and noticed immediately |
| Communicating when you're blocked | It prevents small problems from becoming large ones |
| Asking good questions | Shows you're thinking, not just executing |
| Accepting feedback without defensiveness | Shows you're there to grow, not to be right |
| Finishing things | Half-done work creates more work for others |
| Admitting when you don't know something | Honesty builds more credibility than pretending |

---

### How to Handle Feedback at Work

Feedback is one of the most important inputs you'll receive as a junior engineer. How you respond to it is one of the things your manager watches most closely.

**When you receive feedback:**
- Listen fully before responding — don't interrupt or get defensive
- Ask clarifying questions if something is unclear: "Can you give me an example of what that would look like?"
- Thank them — even if it stings in the moment
- Apply it — and follow up to show you did

**When feedback feels unfair:**
- Wait before reacting — take a day if you need it
- Revisit it with fresh eyes — sometimes it lands differently after reflection
- If you still disagree, have a calm conversation: "I've been thinking about your feedback and I want to make sure I understand it correctly..."

> 💡 **"The engineers who grow fastest are not the ones who make the fewest mistakes. They're the ones who respond best to feedback."**

---

### 90-Day Checklist

- [ ] You can navigate the codebase confidently in at least one area
- [ ] You've shipped at least a few complete, reviewed, and merged PRs
- [ ] You've received feedback and applied it visibly
- [ ] You have a clear understanding of team workflow and norms
- [ ] You've had at least one honest conversation with your manager about your progress
- [ ] You know who to go to for different types of questions
- [ ] You understand the product well enough to explain it to someone outside the company

### How to Prepare

- At the 30, 60, and 90 day marks, write a short self-assessment: what's going well, what's challenging, and what you want to focus on next — share it with your manager proactively
- Ask for feedback explicitly rather than waiting for it: "Is there anything I could be doing differently?" shows maturity and earns respect
- Keep a running list of everything you ship, fix, or improve — this becomes your evidence when performance reviews or raise conversations come up

## 7️⃣ — Growing From Junior to Mid-Level

### What This Is

Getting the job is the beginning of the career, not the goal. Most junior engineers want to reach mid-level — but few have a clear picture of what that actually means or how to get there intentionally.

Mid-level is not just about time served. It is about **how you operate**, not just what you know.

---

### What Actually Separates Junior From Mid-Level

| Junior Engineer | Mid-Level Engineer |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Needs tasks to be clearly defined | Can take a vague problem and define the task themselves |
| Asks for help when stuck | Knows when to dig in and when to ask |
| Focuses on their own work | Aware of how their work fits into the broader system |
| Needs regular check-ins | Works with more autonomy and communicates proactively |
| Learns from feedback | Seeks feedback before it's given |
| Completes assigned work | Takes ownership of outcomes, not just tasks |

The jump from junior to mid-level is fundamentally about **ownership and communication**, not just technical skill.

---

### How to Get There Intentionally

**Own something.**

The fastest way to grow is to take genuine ownership of a part of the system — a feature, a service, a component, a process. Not just "I wrote this code" but "I am the person who understands this area deeply and takes responsibility for it."

**Understand the why, not just the what.**

Mid-level engineers understand why decisions were made, not just what the code does. Ask questions like:
- "Why was this built this way?"
- "What tradeoffs did the team consider?"
- "What would you do differently now?"

**Communicate up, not just across.**

Junior engineers often communicate well with peers but rarely with their manager or senior engineers about their growth goals. Be explicit:
- "I want to take on more ownership of X"
- "I'd like to lead the next feature end-to-end"
- "I'm working on improving my ability to Y — can I get feedback on that specifically?"

**Develop opinions — and be able to defend them.**

Mid-level engineers don't just execute. They contribute to decisions. Practice forming a point of view on technical questions and sharing it respectfully in the right settings.

---

### The 1:1 — Your Most Underused Growth Tool

Regular 1:1 meetings with your manager are one of the most powerful tools you have for growing your career — and most junior engineers waste them by just giving status updates.

Instead, use your 1:1s to:

- Get feedback on specific things you're working on improving
- Talk about your career goals and what you want to take on next
- Raise things that are confusing or frustrating before they become bigger problems
- Ask your manager what they wish you were doing more of

A simple template for every 1:1:

| Topic | Example |
|-------|---------|
| **What I'm working on** | Brief update — not a status report |
| **What I'm proud of** | Something that went well since last time |
| **What I'm finding hard** | Honest about what's challenging |
| **What I want feedback on** | Specific ask for input |
| **What I want to grow into** | Career direction and next steps |

### How to Prepare

- Write down what mid-level means to you specifically — not a generic definition, but what it would look like in your role at your company
- Have an explicit conversation with your manager about the path to mid-level: "What would I need to demonstrate to be considered for the next level?" — then build a plan around the answer
- Find one area of the codebase or product to own deeply over the next 6 months
- Track your contributions — shipped features, bugs fixed, improvements made — so you have evidence when review time comes

## Key Takeaways

The period from offer to mid-level is where careers are actually built. Most guides don't cover this stage — which means most junior engineers navigate it without a map. Having a clear framework for each phase gives you a significant advantage.

---

### The Offer and Onboarding Lifecycle at a Glance

| Stage | What It Is | What You're Building | Common Mistakes |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| 📄 **Evaluate the Offer** | Read and understand all terms | Clarity on what you're agreeing to | Accepting based on salary alone |
| 💬 **Negotiate** | Have a professional ask conversation | Confidence and fair compensation | Not asking at all |
| 🚀 **Prepare to Start** | Set up logistics and mindset | A calm, intentional first day | Cramming tutorials instead of resting |
| 👂 **First Week** | Listen, observe, ask questions | Understanding and early trust | Trying to prove yourself too fast |
| 🔨 **First Month** | Make small, real contributions | Credibility and momentum | Working in isolation when stuck |
| 🤝 **First 90 Days** | Deliver consistently and build relationships | A strong reputation | Waiting for feedback instead of seeking it |
| 📈 **Junior → Mid-Level** | Take ownership, communicate growth | A sustainable career trajectory | Assuming time alone will get you there |

---

### Key Mindset Shifts

| Concept | Common Junior Thought | Reality |
|---------|----------------------|---------|
| 💬 Negotiating | "I might lose the offer" | Companies almost never rescind for a polite ask |
| 🤫 First week | "I should figure things out myself" | Asking good questions builds trust faster than silence |
| 🏃 First month | "I need to prove myself immediately" | Consistency matters more than any one impressive moment |
| ❓ Not knowing things | "I should already know this" | Not knowing things is expected — hiding it is the problem |
| 📣 Feedback | "Criticism means I'm failing" | Feedback is the fastest path to growth |
| ⏳ Mid-level | "It'll happen naturally over time" | Intentional ownership gets you there faster |
| 🗣️ 1:1s | "Just give status updates" | 1:1s are your most powerful career growth tool |

---

### Most Important Takeaways

- 📄 Read the full offer — not just the salary
- 💬 Negotiate — it's expected and almost never backfires
- 👂 Your first week is for listening, not proving
- 🔨 Ship small things completely rather than large things partially
- 🤝 Trust is built through consistency, not brilliance
- 📣 Seek feedback before it's given to you
- 📈 Mid-level is about ownership and communication, not just technical skill
